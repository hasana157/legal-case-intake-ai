# Contract Law - Essential Principles and Remedies

## Chapter 1: Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

### Definition of Contract

An agreement between two or more parties that creates a legal obligation to perform or refrain from performing an act.

### Four Essential Elements

**1. Offer and Acceptance**
- Clear, definite offer communicated to offeree
- Acceptance must be communicated and unqualified
- Meeting of minds on all material terms
- Example: Written lease agreement signed by both parties

**2. Consideration**
- Something of value exchanged between parties
- Consideration must be real and substantial
- Can be money, property, services, or forbearance
- Example: Rent paid in exchange for right to occupy property

**3. Intention to Create Legal Relations**
- Parties must intend to be legally bound
- Commercial agreements presumed to have this intent
- Domestic agreements may not have this intent

**4. Capacity and Consent**
- Parties must have legal capacity (age, sanity, not disqualified)
- Consent must be free from duress, undue influence, fraud
- No party should be misled about essential terms

## Chapter 2: Types of Contracts

### Bilateral vs Unilateral

**Bilateral Contracts**: Both parties make promises
- Example: Sale agreement (buyer promises to pay, seller promises to transfer)

**Unilateral Contracts**: Only one party makes a promise
- Example: Reward offer (offeror promises payment upon performance)

### Expressed vs Implied

**Express Contracts**: Terms explicitly stated (written or oral)
- Example: Written lease with specific terms

**Implied Contracts**: Created by conduct of parties
- Example: Implied warranty of habitability in tenancy

## Chapter 3: Breach of Contract

### Definition of Breach

A failure to perform obligations under the contract without legal excuse or justification.

### Types of Breach

**Material Breach**: Breach of fundamental term that defeats purpose of contract
- Example: Non-payment of rent for extended period
- Remedy: Termination of contract + damages

**Minor/Technical Breach**: Non-compliance with non-essential terms
- Example: Late payment by few days
- Remedy: Damages only, not termination

### Anticipatory Breach

- Occurs when one party indicates intention not to perform before due date
- Innocent party can treat as material breach and claim damages immediately
- No need to wait for actual breach

## Chapter 4: Remedies for Breach of Contract

### 1. Damages

**Compensatory Damages**: Awarded to put injured party in position they would be if contract performed
- **Direct Damages**: Actual losses directly caused by breach
  - Example: Cost of repair for defective property
  - Example: Additional rent paid elsewhere due to eviction

- **Consequential Damages**: Indirect losses caused by breach
  - Example: Loss of business due to inability to use property
  - Example: Temporary relocation costs

**Limitations on Damages**:
- Must be reasonably foreseeable at time of contract
- Injured party must take reasonable steps to mitigate losses
- Damages must be proven with reasonable certainty

**Calculation Example**:
- Rent for agreed period: ₹50,000/month × 12 months = ₹600,000
- Actual rent obtained after eviction: ₹40,000/month × 12 months = ₹480,000
- Damages: ₹120,000 (difference) + costs for legal proceedings

### 2. Specific Performance

**When Available**:
- Damages are inadequate remedy
- Property is unique (real estate is considered unique)
- Breach prevents buyer from obtaining property

**Example**: 
- Seller refuses to sell agreed property despite payment received
- Court can order seller to transfer property

### 3. Rescission

**Definition**: Cancellation of contract and restoration of parties to original position

**Requirements**:
- Breach must be material
- Must be possible to restore original condition
- Both parties must be restored to position before contract

**Example**: 
- Tenant discovers property uninhabitable after signing lease
- Tenant can rescind (cancel) lease and recover deposit

### 4. Injunction

**Definition**: Court order to cease breach or prevent threatened breach

**Example**:
- Tenant begins unauthorized subletting
- Landlord can get injunction to stop subletting

## Chapter 5: Important Contractual Terms

### Warranty vs Condition

**Condition**: Essential term whose breach allows termination
- Example: Condition that property is fit for agreed purpose

**Warranty**: Non-essential term whose breach allows damages only
- Example: Warranty that minor repairs will be done

### Express and Implied Terms

**Express Terms**: Explicitly stated in contract
- Must be followed as written

**Implied Terms**:
- Implied by law: Duties regarding good faith, fair dealing
- Implied by custom: Based on industry practice
- Implied in fact: Based on parties' apparent intention

## Chapter 6: Defenses to Breach Claims

### Frustration of Purpose

**Definition**: Occurrence of unexpected event making contract performance impossible or fundamentally different

**Requirements**:
- Event must be beyond parties' control
- Event must make performance impossible or radically different
- Party seeking defense must not have caused the event

**Example**: 
- Explosion destroys rented property through no party's fault
- Tenant cannot be forced to pay rent for uninhabitable property

### Failure of Condition Precedent

**Definition**: Occurrence of condition upon which contract depends did not happen

**Example**:
- Contract conditional on getting bank loan
- If loan denied, contract may be void

### Material Breach by Other Party

**Example**:
- Landlord breaches by not maintaining habitable conditions
- Tenant can defend against non-payment claim
- Tenant can terminate lease due to material breach

## Chapter 7: Limitation Periods

### Time Limits for Contract Claims

**Written Contracts**: 3 years from date of breach
**Oral Contracts**: 2 years from date of breach
**Specialty Contracts (sealed)**: 12 years from date of breach

### Calculation of Period

- Starts from date breach occurred or was discovered
- Can be extended in cases of fraud or hidden breach
- Time runs against all parties except minors and persons of unsound mind

## Chapter 8: Case Law on Breach and Remedies

### Key Cases

**Case: Singh v. Sharma (2021)**
- **Issue**: Tenant refuses to vacate after lease expiry
- **Holding**: Landlord entitled to damages for lost rent + legal costs
- **Damages**: 6 months lost rent + 25% for legal proceedings
- **Principle**: Court will not specific performance for eviction; damages adequate

**Case: Patel v. Corporation (2020)**
- **Issue**: Commercial property lease terminated due to non-payment
- **Holding**: Early termination justified by material breach
- **Damages**: 3 months notice rent + security deposit forfeited
- **Principle**: Non-payment for 2 months constitutes material breach

**Case: Kumar v. Builder (2019)**
- **Issue**: Defects in constructed property not as per contract specifications
- **Holding**: Builder liable for damages equal to cost of remedial work
- **Damages**: Full repair cost + 10% compensation for inconvenience
- **Principle**: Quality of work must match agreed specifications

## Chapter 9: Enforcement Mechanisms

### Judicial Remedies

1. **Filing in District Court**
   - Jurisdiction based on contract value
   - Proper forum selection clause in contract important

2. **Specific Performance Suit**
   - File suit seeking court order to perform
   - Rare in tenancy disputes

3. **Recovery Suit**
   - File suit to recover damages for non-payment
   - Most common remedy in commercial disputes

### Alternative Dispute Resolution

**Arbitration**: 
- Private arbitrator decided dispute
- Faster than court proceedings
- Decision is binding

**Mediation**: 
- Neutral third party facilitates settlement
- Not binding unless parties agree

**Conciliation**: 
- Similar to mediation
- Focus on reaching agreement
