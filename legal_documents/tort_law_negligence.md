# Tort Law and Negligence - Principles and Cases

## Chapter 1: Fundamental Principles of Tort Law

### Definition of Tort

A tort is a civil wrong (other than breach of contract) that causes harm and creates legal liability for the person who commits the wrongful act, called a tortfeasor.

### Key Elements of Tort Law

1. **Duty of Care**: Existence of legal duty towards the plaintiff
2. **Breach of Duty**: Failure to exercise reasonable care
3. **Causation**: Breach must directly or indirectly cause the harm
4. **Damages**: Harm or loss suffered by the plaintiff

---

## Chapter 2: Negligence

### Definition of Negligence

Negligence is the failure to exercise the standard of care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances. It is a breach of duty owed to another which results in damage.

### Three Essential Elements of Negligence

**Element 1: Duty of Care**
- A legal obligation to act with reasonable care
- Examples:
  - Owners of property have duty to maintain premises safely
  - Professionals have duty to provide services with professional competence
  - Drivers have duty to operate vehicles safely

**Element 2: Breach of Duty**
- Failure to meet the standard of reasonable care
- Examples:
  - Not maintaining electrical wiring in a commercial property
  - Speeding in a residential area
  - Failing to warn of known hazards

**Element 3: Causation and Damages**
- The breach must cause direct or indirect harm
- The plaintiff must suffer actual damage (physical, emotional, financial)

### Standard of Care

**Reasonable Person Test**: Would a reasonable person of ordinary prudence have acted similarly under the same circumstances?

**Factors Considered**:
- Nature of activity
- Risk involved
- Utility of conduct
- Burden of precaution
- Custom and practice in the industry

## Chapter 3: Liability for Property Damage

### Section 5: Duty to Maintain Property

**5.1 Landlord's Liability**:
- Landlord is liable for maintaining common areas
- Landlord is liable for structural defects
- Landlord must repair or replace dangerous conditions
- Landlord is liable if injury results from negligent maintenance

**5.2 Negligent Maintenance Cases**:

**Case Study: Negligent Electrical Wiring**
- **Facts**: Shop owner fails to maintain electrical wiring, causing fire
- **Negligence**: Failure to inspect, test, or replace deteriorated wiring
- **Liability**: Shop owner is liable for damages to neighboring properties
- **Damages**: Property damage, loss of business, personal injury

**5.3 Causation Analysis**:
- Direct causation: Property owner's negligence directly caused the fire
- Foreseeability: Fire damage was a foreseeable result of poor wiring
- Proximate cause: Direct and uninterrupted chain of causation

## Chapter 4: Negligent Acts and Omissions

### Acts of Negligence

**Active Negligence**: Doing something that causes harm
- Examples: Leaving hazardous materials unattended, operating machinery unsafely

**Negligent Omission**: Failure to do something required
- Examples: Failing to repair known hazards, failing to warn of dangers

### Liability for Third Party Negligence

**Vicarious Liability**: Employer/property owner liable for employee's negligence
- **Requirement**: Employee must be acting within scope of employment
- **Application**: Homeowner liable if employee negligently causes fire

## Chapter 5: Damages in Negligence Cases

### Types of Damages Recoverable

**Compensatory Damages**:
- Direct damages: Actual property damage (repairs, replacement)
- Consequential damages: Loss of use, business interruption
- Personal injury damages: Medical expenses, pain and suffering
- Economic losses: Lost income, decreased property value

**Calculation of Property Damage**:
1. Cost of repair or replacement
2. Market value reduction of property
3. Loss of rental income during repairs
4. Cost of alternative accommodation
5. Cost of salvaging usable items

### Case Example: Fire Damage Settlement

**Scenario**: Fire spreads from commercial property to residential property
- **Direct Damage**: Repair cost of burned structure
- **Indirect Damage**: Cost of temporary shelter, relocation
- **Consequential Damage**: Loss of business during repairs, decreased property value
- **Mitigation Duty**: Plaintiff must take reasonable steps to minimize losses

## Chapter 6: Defenses to Negligence

### Common Defenses

**Assumption of Risk**: Plaintiff knowingly accepted the risk
- Example: Living next to a factory knowing risk of explosion

**Contributory Negligence**: Plaintiff's own negligence contributed to harm
- Example: Failure to report known hazard

**Comparative Negligence**: Damages reduced by plaintiff's percentage of fault
- Formula: Plaintiff's damages × (100% - Plaintiff's % of fault)

**Act of God**: Harm caused by unforeseeable natural event
- Does not apply to negligent failure to prepare for natural events

## Chapter 7: Burden and Standard of Proof

### In Civil Cases

**Standard of Proof**: Balance of Probabilities
- Plaintiff must prove case is more likely true than not (> 50%)
- Lower threshold than criminal standard (beyond reasonable doubt)

**Burden of Proof**:
- Plaintiff bears burden of proving negligence
- Defendant bears burden of proving defenses

### Evidence Required

**Direct Evidence**:
- Photographs, videos of the incident
- Witness testimony
- Expert reports on causation

**Circumstantial Evidence**:
- Pattern of negligent behavior
- Maintenance records showing lack of upkeep
- Industry standards comparison

## Chapter 8: Recent Precedents

### Leading Case Law on Property Negligence

**Case: Sharma v. Patel (2021)**
- **Issue**: Negligent maintenance of electrical system in commercial property
- **Holding**: Property owner is strictly liable for fires caused by negligent maintenance
- **Damages Awarded**: Full cost of repairs plus 15% for loss of business
- **Principle**: Duty of care extends to neighboring properties

**Case: Kumar v. Housing Society (2020)**
- **Issue**: Negligent construction/maintenance of shared electrical infrastructure
- **Holding**: Building/society management is liable for injuries caused by negligence
- **Damages Awarded**: Compensatory damages for injury + punitive damages for gross negligence

**Case: Chatterjee v. Municipal Authority (2019)**
- **Issue**: Negligent failure to maintain public property standards
- **Holding**: Public entities have heightened duty of care
- **Damages Awarded**: Full restoration damages + damages for inconvenience

## Chapter 9: Important Provisions and Timeframes

### Limitation Period

**For Property Damage Claims**:
- 3 years from date of damage (from when damage was discovered)
- Can be extended in case of hidden defects

**For Personal Injury Claims**:
- 3 years from date of injury
- Can be extended up to 12 years in some cases

### Notification Requirements

- Plaintiff should notify defendant promptly
- Claims should be filed within prescribed period
- Delay may affect damages awarded
